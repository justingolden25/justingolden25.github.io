Who I Am

My name is Justin Golden, and I'm a first-year Electrical Engineering student at the Georgia Institute of Technology — one of the most rigorous and respected engineering programs in the country. Although I'm in my first year on campus, I came in with sophomore-level credits, which has allowed me to dive deeper into the technical curriculum earlier than most of my peers.

Growing up, I was always the person taking things apart to see how they worked — whether it was a remote-controlled car, a broken laptop, or an old radio from a garage sale. That curiosity eventually became a direction: electrical engineering sits at the heart of nearly every modern technology, from the smartphones in our pockets to the power grids that light our cities, and I want to be part of shaping the next generation of those systems.

Before Georgia Tech, I had the opportunity to conduct research at Columbia University, an experience that gave me an early taste of real scientific inquiry, rigorous methodology, and the collaborative culture of an academic lab. That experience confirmed what I suspected: I'm driven not just by engineering as a profession, but by the pursuit of understanding itself.

03 / Projects

Work & Research

GT Coursework

Analog Circuit Design — Op-Amp Filter

Designed and simulated a multi-stage active bandpass filter using operational amplifiers as part of ECE 2040 (Circuit Analysis). The filter was specified to pass a 1–5 kHz band for audio processing applications with less than 3 dB of passband ripple. Work included hand analysis, LTspice simulation, and a physical breadboard build verified on an oscilloscope.

LTspice Op-Amps Filter Design ECE 2040
Code / Report Details
GT Coursework

Embedded Systems — STM32 Sensor Node

Programmed an STM32F4 microcontroller in C to read temperature and humidity data from a DHT22 sensor, display live readings on an I²C OLED screen, and transmit data over UART to a PC terminal. The project introduced key embedded concepts including GPIO configuration, interrupt-driven I/O, and low-power sleep modes. Board design was completed in KiCad.

STM32 C I²C / UART KiCad

Personal

DSP Toolkit — Python Signal Library

Built a personal Python library for digital signal processing experiments, featuring Butterworth, Chebyshev, and FIR filter implementations alongside FFT analysis and spectrogram visualization tools. The project grew out of a desire to understand DSP concepts at the implementation level, not just through pre-built library calls. Documentation and unit tests are included, and the project is open-sourced on GitHub.
