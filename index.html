Who I Am

My name is Justin Golden, and I'm a first-year Electrical Engineering student at the Georgia Institute of Technology. I came in with sophomore-level credits, which has let me dive deeper into the technical curriculum earlier than most — but honestly, my curiosity about how things work has always moved faster than any curriculum could.

I'm drawn to electrical engineering because it sits at the heart of nearly every modern technology, and I want to be part of shaping what comes next. My focus is on clean energy and the role electrical systems will play in fighting climate change. I believe it's the defining challenge of my generation, and that weight doesn't intimidate me — it motivates me.

Outside the classroom, I need to stay active to stay balanced. I box, lift weights, play basketball, and recently picked up golf — though my short game has a long way to go. I've been playing piano since I was seven, and after more than a decade of daily practice, it's become less of a hobby and more of a language I think in. I picked up guitar more recently and love how different the two instruments feel. I also spend time reading, following markets and learning about personal investing, and getting outside to hike whenever I can.

I'm a firm believer that the best engineers are well-rounded humans first. Curiosity, empathy, and resilience — the same qualities that make someone a good teammate, a good teacher, or a good friend — are the ones that make someone a good engineer. I try to bring all of those into everything I do.

Self-Tightening Shoe — Assistive Device for Arthritis & Neuropathy

Designed and prototyped a self-tightening shoe to assist individuals with arthritis and neuropathy, reducing the risk of falls from untied laces. I programmed an Arduino microcontroller to drive a servo motor that tightened and loosened laces in response to button input, and integrated a custom 3D-printed crank mechanism to provide the mechanical leverage needed. Research

Columbia University — PDMS Research (Kymissis Lab)

Conducted 400+ hours of undergraduate research at Columbia University's Kymissis Lab over two summers, investigating polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) for applications in robotics and electronic skin. My first summer focused on surface property modification — treating PDMS samples with various silanes to enhance hydrophobicity for robotic manipulation applications, using a contact angle goniometer and ImageJ analysis to quantify results. I served as lead author of a research paper submitted to the Journal of Vacuum Science & Technology B. My second summer shifted toward electrical characterization, where I designed and fabricated custom PCBs to evaluate conductivity of PDMS-based composites through Transmission Line Method and Van der Pauw testing.
